## Pain Signal Decision Policy

This prototype uses a confidence threshold of **0.7** as the gate for writing pain signals back to HubSpot. I chose 0.7 because it balances **recall** and **precision** for a GTM workflow: below that point, the model is often “seeing something interesting” but not strongly enough to risk polluting CRM data with weak or spurious signals. Above 0.7, we are intentionally biasing toward **high‑quality, sales‑actionable insights** even if that means we miss a few borderline opportunities. In a real implementation, I’d validate or tune this threshold by sampling model outputs with reps and marketing partners, but 0.7 is a reasonable default that “feels” like strong but not perfect conviction.

The decision gate has two branches. **If `confidence >= 0.7`**, we treat the model’s output as actionable: we write the pain signal properties back to the company record (e.g., detected signal types, a normalized score, and a short explanation) and optionally trigger downstream workflows such as tasks for owners or inclusion in a GTM campaign list. **If `confidence < 0.7`**, we update hubspot with bdr_manual_review for internal analytics. This keeps the CRM clean and avoids creating noise for reps, while still allowing us to capture lower‑confidence signals elsewhere if we want to analyze model behavior over time.

There are clear limitations to this approach in the current project. All of the news content is **mocked data**, and I’m not actually calling a real News API or applying any ranking, de‑duplication, or time‑based relevance filters. That means the confidence score and pain‑signal detection are being exercised on a small, hand‑crafted sample rather than on the messy, sometimes contradictory stream of real healthcare news. Additionally, because there is no human‑in‑the‑loop review step here, the chosen threshold is based on intuition and general best practices, not on empirical evaluation in production.

With real data, this policy could become much stronger. Integrating a real news source (e.g., NewsAPI or a healthcare‑specific feed) would allow us to test the model on **larger, more diverse article sets**, measure precision/recall against human‑labeled examples, and then **tune the 0.7 threshold** (or even make it segment‑specific by region, payer mix, or health system size). We could also enrich the decision logic with recency, source credibility, and the number of corroborating articles, and add a lightweight review queue where BDRs validate borderline signals. Over time, that feedback loop would let us retrain prompts, adjust scoring, and harden this from a demo into a reliable GTM signal engine.
